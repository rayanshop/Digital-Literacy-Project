# Stay Safe Online – My Prevention Checklist
### Things Every College Student in India Should Know
**Name:** Rayansh Jaiswal | **Reg. No.:** 25MIM10077 | **Branch:** Integrated M.Tech AI

---

Honestly, most of us think cybercrime happens to other people — older relatives, less "tech-savvy" folks. But the more I researched this topic, the more I realised that college students are actually one of the biggest targets. We're online constantly, we use UPI daily, and we're always looking for internships, scholarships, and deals. That's exactly what scammers count on.

Here's a checklist I put together — things I genuinely think we should all be doing.

---

## The Checklist

- [ ] **1. Stop and think before you click anything**
  I know it sounds obvious, but in the moment — when a message says "your account will be blocked in 2 hours" — your brain panics and your fingers just tap. That's the whole trick. Take a breath. Go to the website yourself by typing the URL. Don't follow any link from a message.

- [ ] **2. Actually look at the URL before you type your password**
  The fake websites look shockingly real. The only giveaway is usually the URL — something like `sbi-secure-update.com` instead of `onlinesbi.com`. Make it a habit: before you enter anything, glance at the address bar. Make sure it says `https://` and the domain is exactly right.

- [ ] **3. Turn on 2-Step Verification everywhere**
  This one is genuinely a lifesaver. Even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without the OTP that comes to your phone. Set it up on Gmail, Instagram, your college portal, everything. It takes two minutes and can save you from a nightmare.

- [ ] **4. Stop using the same password for everything**
  I know it's tempting. But if one account gets hacked, they try the same password everywhere — your email, your bank, your everything. Use a different password for each important account. If that sounds like a lot, just get Bitwarden — it's free and remembers them all for you. Even a passphrase like `Chai#Baarish@Mumbai2025` is way better than `rayansh123`.

- [ ] **5. Don't log into important apps on public Wi-Fi**
  The free Wi-Fi at the canteen, the café near college, the railway station — these are hunting grounds for attackers. Avoid opening your banking app or even your email on these networks. Just switch to mobile data. It costs a few MBs but keeps you safe.

- [ ] **6. Be suspicious of anything that sounds too good**
  "You've been selected for a ₹50,000 scholarship — register now." "Work from home, earn ₹800 per hour — send your Aadhaar to apply." These messages target students because we're often looking for exactly these opportunities. If an offer came out of nowhere and wants your personal details or a small "registration fee" upfront — it's a scam. Every single time.

- [ ] **7. Keep your phone and apps updated**
  I used to ignore those update notifications for weeks. Turns out, those updates patch security holes that hackers are actively trying to exploit. Just update regularly. It's annoying for two minutes and protective for months.

- [ ] **8. Be careful what you share on Instagram and LinkedIn**
  Your phone number, your college roll number, your hometown, your date of birth — all of this sitting publicly on your profile gives scammers exactly what they need to write a convincing, personalised phishing message. Tighten your privacy settings and don't post everything.

## The Two UPI Tips That Really Matter 💳

- [ ] **9. Your UPI PIN is yours alone — nobody else ever needs it**
  This is the big one. No bank employee, no Google Pay support, no government officer, absolutely no one will ever legitimately ask for your UPI PIN or OTP. Not over call, not over WhatsApp, not ever. The moment someone asks for it, hang up. Also — and this trips a lot of people up — **you never need to enter your PIN to receive money.** If someone's telling you to enter your PIN to "accept" a payment, they're stealing from you.

- [ ] **10. Read the UPI confirmation screen before you hit Pay**
  Scammers create UPI IDs that sound official — things like `pm-relief@upi` or `sbi.cashback@ybl`. The app will show you the registered name before you confirm. Read it. If the name doesn't match who you think you're paying, cancel immediately. Five seconds of checking can save your entire month's budget.

## If Something Does Happen — Here's Where to Go

Nobody plans to get scammed. But if it happens to you or someone you know, act fast — the first few hours matter most.

| Where to Go | What to Do |
|---|---|
| **Helpline 1930** | Call this immediately after any financial fraud. They can try to freeze the transaction before the money moves. |
| **cybercrime.gov.in** | File an official complaint online, any time of day. Keep your transaction IDs and screenshots ready. |
| **Your Bank's Helpline** | Call them to block your card or account if you've shared any sensitive details. |
| **Local Police Station** | For serious cases, file an FIR under the IT Act, 2000. |

> The faster you call **1930**, the better the chances of getting your money back. Don't feel embarrassed — call first, feel embarrassed later (or not at all, it can happen to anyone).

## My Reflection

When I started researching phishing, I expected to find a bunch of obviously fake emails that only gullible people fall for. What actually surprised me was how wrong I was. Modern phishing attacks are so carefully designed — right logos, right language, right urgency — that even people who work in IT have been fooled. Reading about cases where educated, aware individuals lost lakhs simply because they were stressed or in a hurry made me realise that this isn't about being "dumb." It's about being human.

The one habit I'm going to change is simple but hard: I'm going to stop reacting to urgency in digital messages. Any message that tells me to act "immediately" or "within 24 hours" is now automatically suspicious to me. I'll pause, verify through the official website or helpline number, and only then decide what to do. That two-minute pause might be the most important security habit I can build.
